What Is Reinsurance?

Insurance for insurance companies

Reinsurance is when an insurance company (the cedant or ceding company) transfers some of its risk to another company (the reinsurer).

Why Reinsurance Exists

  • Capacity: Enable primary insurers to write more business
  • Volatility: Smooth out claims experience
  • Catastrophe: Protection against low-frequency, high-severity events
  • Capital: More efficient use of regulatory capital
  • Expertise: Access to specialized knowledge

Key Terms You'll Use Daily

  • Cedant: The insurance company ceding risk
  • Ceding commission: Payment from reinsurer to cedant for acquisition costs
  • Retrocession: Reinsurance purchased by a reinsurer
  • Loss ratio: Claims / Premiums
  • Combined ratio: (Claims + Expenses) / Premiums

Why IFRS 17/9 Exist: The IFRS 4 Disaster

Historical Context: IFRS 4 (2005-2023) was a temporary standard. It allowed insurers to continue using their local accounting practices. Result? Complete chaos.

IFRS 4 Problems

  • Zero comparability between companies
  • Twenty different ways to measure reserves
  • Investment returns in P&L, liabilities at historical cost
  • No concept of CSM or profit emergence
  • Gaming through reserve releases
  • Analysts couldn't value insurance companies reliably
  • Acquisition costs treated inconsistently
  • No market-consistent valuation

IFRS 17/9 Solutions

  • Single global measurement approach
  • Current discount rates for liabilities
  • Explicit CSM for profit over time
  • Risk adjustment for uncertainty
  • Consistent recognition patterns
  • Asset-liability management transparency
  • Investment accounting aligned (IFRS 9)
  • Mandatory detailed disclosures
2004: IASB starts insurance accounting project
2005: IFRS 4 issued as "temporary" standard
2017: IFRS 17 finally issued (12 years later)
2020: Delayed from 2021 to 2023 (industry pressure)
Jan 1, 2023: IFRS 17 effective date (finally!)
2024-2025: First audited annual reports under IFRS 17

Who's adopted, who's not, and why it matters

Countries/Regions Using IFRS 17 (as of 2025)

  • Switzerland: Swiss Re, Zurich, etc.

  • Most IFRS jurisdictions: 140+ countries

Major Markets NOT Using IFRS 17

  • United States: Uses US GAAP (Targeted Improvements, LDTI). Will NOT adopt IFRS 17. This creates reconciliation headaches for European reinsurers with US operations.
  • Bermuda: Leading reinsurance domicile, uses Statutory accounting. Many Bermuda reinsurers produce IFRS 17 for parent company consolidation.
  • China: Uses China GAAP, moving toward convergence but not full adoption.

How Reinsurance Accounting Differs from Primary Insurance

Reinsurers have a unique position: they hold reinsurance contracts (assets) AND issue reinsurance contracts (liabilities). This dual role creates complexity.

Unearned Premium $XXX
Claims Liabilities $XXX

Unique Reinsurer Challenges

  • Claims data lag (cedants report months later)
  • Counterparty credit risk (both cedants and retros)
  • Multiple layers of risk transfer
  • Portfolio concentration (few large cedants)
  • Information asymmetry (reliant on cedant data)

Primary Insurer Differences

  • Direct policyholder relationship
  • Immediate claims knowledge
  • Reinsurance is their asset, not liability
  • Ceding commission income (not expense)
  • Smaller average policy sizes

Section 2: IFRS 17 Core

IFRS 17: The Building Block Philosophy

Why IFRS 17 is structured the way it is

Core Philosophy: Insurance contracts are measured by building up from cash flows, not by referring to premiums charged or historical cost. Think "mark-to-model" not "mark-to-market".
Insurance Contract Liability = Three Building Blocks
1. Fulfilment Cash Flows (FCF)
   = Present Value of Future Cash Flows
   + Risk Adjustment for Non-Financial Risk

2. Contractual Service Margin (CSM)
   = Unearned profit locked in at inception

Total Liability = FCF + CSM

What This Means

  • Premium received is NOT revenue immediately
  • Liabilities remeasured each reporting period
  • Profit emerges as service is provided
  • Future cash outflows are explicitly estimated
  • Uncertainty has a cost (risk adjustment)

Departure from Old World

  • NOT "premium in, reserve out"
  • NOT historical cost accounting
  • NOT "whatever local regulator says"
  • NOT profit recognition at inception
  • NOT hiding losses in reserves
Why CSM Exists: IFRS 17 prohibits Day 1 profit recognition. If your best estimate cash flows show you'll make money, that profit is deferred in the CSM and released over the coverage period. This prevents gaming and smooths earnings.

Section 2: IFRS 17 Core

Contractual Service Margin (CSM): The Heart of IFRS 17

What CSM Represents: The unearned profit on a group of contracts. It's the "deferred Day 1 gain" that gets released into P&L as the reinsurer provides coverage.
CSM at Initial Recognition (profitable contracts)
CSM = - (Estimates of Future Cash Flows + Risk Adjustment)

If this calculation is negative (expected profit), CSM = that amount
If positive (expected loss), CSM = 0, and Loss Component created
CSM Subsequent Measurement (Simplified)
Opening CSM
+ Interest accretion (locked-in rate)
+ New contracts added to group
± Changes in future cash flow estimates
± Experience adjustments (favorable/unfavorable)
- CSM RELEASED TO P&L (coverage units)
= Closing CSM

CSM for Reinsurance ISSUED

  • Represents profit we'll earn as reinsurer
  • Released as we provide coverage to cedants
  • Coverage units: typically premium-weighted or risk-weighted
  • Large CSM = profitable book
  • Released faster if claims emerge early

CSM for Reinsurance HELD

  • Represents expected benefit from our retrocession
  • Released as retrocessionaire provides coverage to us
  • Mirrors the CSM pattern of underlying business
  • Reduces as we receive claims recoveries
  • Yes, assets can have CSM! (This confuses people)
Common Mistake: Thinking CSM is a "reserve." It's not. It's deferred profit, not a provision for claims. Claims are in the Fulfilment Cash Flows. CSM + FCF = Total Liability.
Section 3: Reinsurance Held (Assets)

Treaty Proportional Held: Quota Share Example

Full lifecycle accounting from inception through claim recovery

Scenario: We (the reinsurer) purchase 40% QS retrocession on our Life term portfolio. Treaty covers €100M of our exposure. We pay €8M annual premium. Retrocessionaire pays us a 20% ceding commission.
Day 1: Inception
  • Estimate future recovery cash flows: €30M (PV €25M)
  • Estimate future premium payments: €40M (PV €35M)
  • Risk adjustment: €2M
  • FCF = €25M - €35M - €2M = -€12M (net outflow)
  • CSM created: €12M (expected benefit from contract)
  • Ceding commission received: €1.6M → reduces initial FCF
Year 1: Normal Operations
  • CSM released proportionally as coverage provided
  • Premium paid to retrocessionaire: recognized as incurred
  • No major claims → FCF updates minimal
  • Interest accretion on CSM
Year 3: Large Mortality Event
  • Underlying life policies pay €5M in death claims
  • We recover 40% = €2M from retrocessionaire
  • Recovery booked as reduction in insurance service expense
  • Reinsurance asset increases for amounts receivable
  • CSM continues to amortize (unaffected by paid claims)
Year 5: Adverse Development
  • Mortality experience worse than expected
  • Increase future recovery estimates
  • Favorable change → increases CSM (more benefit from retro)
  • Released to P&L over remaining coverage period
Balance Sheet Impact (Simplified)
Asset Side:
Reinsurance Contract Asset = FCF + CSM
= (PV of Recoveries - PV of Premiums + Risk Adj) + CSM

Asset increases when: recoveries increase, premiums decrease, CSM grows
Asset decreases when: CSM released, premiums paid, recoveries received

Section 5: IFRS 9 for Reinsurers

Expected Credit Loss (ECL): Reinsurer-Specific Challenges

Reinsurers face credit risk on TWO fronts: cedants (premium receivables) and retrocessionaires (recovery receivables). IFRS 9 requires Expected Credit Loss provisions on both.

ECL Three-Stage Model
Stage 1: Performing (no significant credit deterioration)
→ Provision = 12-month Expected Credit Loss

Stage 2: Significant increase in credit risk
→ Provision = Lifetime Expected Credit Loss

Stage 3: Credit-impaired (default/bankruptcy)
→ Provision = Lifetime ECL + interest income on net carrying amount

Premium Receivables (Cedants)

  • Exposure: Outstanding premiums due from cedants
  • Risk: Cedant financial distress or insolvency
  • Indicators: Credit rating downgrades, payment delays, regulatory action
  • Measurement: Use cedant credit ratings, loss-given-default, exposure at default
  • Challenge: Offsetting payables (claims we owe them)

Reinsurance Recoverables (Retros)

  • Exposure: Future claim recoveries from retrocessionaires
  • Risk: Retrocessionaire can't pay when cat hits
  • Indicators: Retro's capital adequacy, rating, claims-paying history
  • Measurement: Probability of default × Loss given default × Exposure
  • Challenge: Long-tail: default might happen in 10 years
Key Question: ECL vs Risk Adjustment – aren't we double-counting credit risk?

Answer: They're different!
• Risk Adjustment (IFRS 17): Non-financial risk (actuarial uncertainty, claims volatility)
• ECL (IFRS 9): Credit/counterparty risk (will they pay us?)

BUT: The boundary is fuzzy and causes interpretation debates. IASB says "they're separate" but firms struggle with this in practice.
Click to reveal: Worked Example - Retrocessionaire ECL Calculation →
Scenario: We have $50M in retrocession recoverables from Retro X (rated BBB).

Inputs:
• Probability of Default (PD): 1.5% over 5 years
• Loss Given Default (LGD): 60% (we'd recover 40% in bankruptcy)
• Exposure at Default (EAD): $50M

Calculation:
ECL = PD × LGD × EAD
ECL = 1.5% × 60% × $50M = $450,000

Accounting:
Dr. ECL Expense $450K
Cr. Allowance for ECL $450K

Balance sheet shows net: $50M - $450K = $49.55M
Section 6: The Intersection

Where IFRS 17 and IFRS 9 Collide

Accounting mismatches and P&L volatility

The Core Problem: IFRS 17 measures insurance liabilities using insurance-specific discount rates. IFRS 9 measures financial assets at fair value or amortized cost using market rates. These don't match. Result? P&L volatility that has nothing to do with your actual business performance.

The Mismatch Diagram

ASSETS (IFRS 9)

  • Bonds: Fair value through P&L
  • Market interest rates rise
  • Bond values fall → Loss in P&L
  • Reinsurance recoverables: ECL recognized
≠
Measurement
Mismatch!

LIABILITIES (IFRS 17)

  • Insurance contracts: Locked-in rates for CSM
  • Market rates rise
  • Liability falls → Gain goes to OCI (not P&L!)
  • Risk adjustment: Non-financial
P&L RESULT:
Asset loss in P&L, liability gain in OCI → artificial P&L volatility → CFO gets angry calls from analysts → you explain this quarterly

IFRS 17 Discount Rate Choices

  • Top-down: Start with asset returns, adjust for credit/liquidity
  • Bottom-up: Risk-free rate + illiquidity premium
  • Locked-in rate for CSM changes
  • Current rate for FCF discount
  • OCI option available (to reduce P&L vol)

Practical Solutions Firms Use

  • Elect OCI for insurance finance I/E
  • Hedge accounting for asset/liability matching
  • FVTPL designation for bonds to match liabilities
  • Dedicated reporting: "underlying profit" vs "IFRS profit"
  • Extensive disclosures explaining movements
